# 03 — Problem Statement

## The Problem Statement

---

### The Customer Segment

**Discovery-oriented urban premium consumers, aged 25–38, based in Ireland, the UK, Germany, and the Netherlands.**

Psychographic profile: they have abandoned mass tourism. They do not want Barcelona again. They subscribe to Spotify, HelloFresh, and at least one craft drinks box. They spend €80–120 per month on premium food and beverage experiences. They take 2–3 holidays per year and report — in survey after survey — wanting "authentic," "off-the-beaten-track" travel. They are active on Instagram and TikTok, where the social currency of finding something *no one else has found yet* is the highest status signal available.

Behavioural signature: they research destinations online for hours, then default to the same five overexposed cities anyway — because no trusted, *engaging* mechanism exists to help them find anywhere else.

---

### The Core Pain Point

**This consumer has the intent to discover hidden Spain. They have no mechanism to do it.**

The discovery problem is not one of motivation — it is one of infrastructure. Authentic coastal fishing villages, inland medieval towns, and historically significant coves exist in abundance across Spain's 8,000km of coastline and 17 autonomous regions. But they are invisible to the international consumer for a structural reason: tourism demand is a self-reinforcing popularity loop. The most-visited places generate the most content, which generates the most visibility, which generates the most visits. Places outside that loop stay outside it — permanently.

This consumer is trapped: they want the undiscovered place, but every tool they use to find travel recommendations (Instagram, TripAdvisor, Google Travel, travel media) is algorithmically optimised to show them what is already popular. The result is that they go to Barcelona for the third time, or they go to Mallorca, and they feel vaguely cheated — and hundreds of Spanish coastal villages receive no economic benefit from tourism at all.

---

### Why Existing Solutions Fail

| Solution | Why It Fails |
|---|---|
| **Turespaña (Spain's national tourism board)** | Bureaucratic, untargeted, passive. Content is broadcast, not engaging. No mechanic that motivates action or discovery. No personalisation. |
| **TripAdvisor / Google Travel** | Entirely review-driven — popularity compounds popularity. Undiscovered places have no reviews, therefore no visibility, therefore remain undiscovered. |
| **Travel blogs and influencer content** | Ad-incentivised to cover accessible, photogenic, already-popular locations. No financial model for covering genuinely obscure places. |
| **Airbnb Experiences** | Curated by existing local hosts, not by intent to discover new regions. Concentrated in cities and established tourist areas. |
| **Subscription drinks boxes** | Deliver taste, novelty, and convenience — but zero cultural depth, zero geographic story, zero reason for the consumer to form a relationship with a place. |

No existing product sits in the intersection of *premium beverage consumption* and *geographic discovery*. The gap is not incremental — it is structural.

---

### The Market and Societal Cost of Inaction

**For the consumer:** a growing segment of high-intent, high-spending travellers continues to be mis-served, defaulting to repeat visits to saturated destinations they do not prefer, generating dissatisfaction and missed personal experience.

**For rural Spain:** the economic cost is severe and compounding. Spain's *España Vaciada* ("Emptied Spain") phenomenon has seen over 3,000 rural municipalities classified at serious risk of demographic collapse, with young people leaving for cities and tourism income that could have sustained communities flowing exclusively to coastal resorts and urban centres instead (INE, 2023). Cultural heritage sites in these regions — including documented corsair anchorages, fishing cooperative histories, and pre-modern maritime trade infrastructure — are deteriorating for lack of economic activity and institutional attention.

**For the EU:** Spain's overtourism crisis is generating measurable political instability in receiving cities. Barcelona, Seville, and the Canary Islands have each seen organised anti-tourism protests in 2024, with residents citing unaffordable housing, displacement, and cultural erosion. The EU's European Tourism Indicator System (ETIS) has explicitly identified tourism concentration as a structural risk to the sustainability of European tourism as a whole.

---

### The Validating Data

**1.** Spain received **85.1 million international tourists in 2023** (INE, 2024) — a record. Yet the top five destinations (Catalonia, the Balearics, the Canary Islands, Andalusia's coastal strip, and Madrid) accounted for **over 70% of all overnight stays** (Frontur/Estadística de Movimientos Turísticos en Fronteras, INE). The remaining 12 autonomous regions — home to thousands of historically and culturally significant communities — shared less than 30% of economic benefit from a sector worth €175 billion to the Spanish economy.

**2.** The European ready-to-drink and canned cocktail market was valued at approximately **€3.8–4.2 billion in 2023 and is growing at 15–28% annually** (IWSR Beverage Alcohol Report, 2024), with premiumisation — consumers trading up for story, provenance, and experience over commodity taste — identified as the primary growth driver. Subscription beverage D2C is the fastest-growing channel within this segment, yet **no brand in the category has deployed a location-discovery or cultural heritage mechanic.** The commercial white space is unoccupied.

---

### One-Sentence Summary

A high-value, experience-hungry consumer in Northern Europe cannot find hidden Spain — not because it does not exist, but because every tool built to help them look is structurally incapable of showing them anything that is not already popular — and the communities that would benefit most from being found have no means to be discovered.
