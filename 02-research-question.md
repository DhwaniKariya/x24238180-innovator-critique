# 02 — Research Question

## Research Question

**To what extent can gamified, narrative-embedded consumer products redirect tourist demand toward economically marginalised coastal heritage destinations in Spain, and what role does product-embedded storytelling play in converting consumer curiosity into physical visitation?**

---

## Academic Domain and Researchability

This question sits at the intersection of **experiential marketing**, **tourism economics**, and **consumer behaviour** — specifically engaging with the literature on curiosity-gap theory (Loewenstein, 1994), destination image formation (Baloglu & McCleary, 1999), and the emerging field of phygital (physical-digital) product experience design. It is forward-looking in its relevance to the EU's tourism diversification agenda and Spain's documented overtourism paradox, in which over 65% of 85 million annual visitors concentrate in five cities while thousands of coastal heritage sites remain economically invisible (INE, 2024; UNWTO, 2019).

The question is fully researchable using existing data and methodology: tourism intention can be measured via the adapted Theory of Planned Behaviour scale (Ajzen, 1991); destination awareness shifts are quantifiable through pre/post survey design with a subscriber cohort; and physical visitation impact is measurable via regional footfall counter data already collected by Spain's autonomous community tourism boards. Spain's position as Europe's largest watermelon producer (Ministerio de Agricultura, 2023) and one of the continent's richest maritime heritage archives provides a uniquely data-rich context in which to test the thesis that a single consumer product category — premium canned beverages — can function as a private-sector destination marketing mechanism.

---

## Why This Question Matters

The core tension in the founding story — Fermín's hidden chart, the unnamed corsair routes, the place that exists but is never shown — is also the structural gap in Spain's tourism economy: the most culturally significant places are the least visited, not because they lack value, but because no scalable discovery mechanism exists to connect them to willing consumers. This research question tests whether that mechanism can be a can of watermelon cocktail.
