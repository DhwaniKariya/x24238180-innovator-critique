# 01 — Story

In Cádiz, where the Atlantic shoulders itself against the old city walls like something that has not forgotten a grievance, August arrives as a verdict — white light, white stone, the smell of salt and cut *sandía* drifting up from the harbour where old Fermín sat each midday with his knife and his patience, the watermelon juice darkening the dock timbers beneath his boots while the heat pressed down on the rigging and the anchored boats and the long, indifferent sea beyond. From somewhere in the tangled line above him, the monk parakeet — green and theatrical, a squatter from another continent who had claimed this vessel as his territory three seasons ago and could not be persuaded otherwise — screamed once, sharp as a blade, and then went silent; and Fermín did not look up, because he had learned to read that bird the way his grandfather taught him to read weather, not by watching it but by listening for when it stopped. The tourists who wandered the old quarter photographed the parakeets as something charming, something Mediterranean, but Fermín knew that this coast had always been a place where things arrived that had no business arriving — the corsairs had understood this, the ones who tracked the silver galleons through the Strait and into the Atlantic dark, whose flags were not symbols of romance but of a calculated, patient terror, and whose trade routes, if the old charts were to be believed, had never been fully mapped by any authority that wanted the answer known. He kept the map in the lining of his jacket, the one his father had kept in the lining of his, a hand-drawn thing that marked not fish but something heavier, still resting on the seafloor twelve miles west of the lighthouse — and as the parakeet screamed again, twice this time, which meant a boat was coming in from open water, Fermín looked up and saw, rounding the headland from the west, a vessel he did not recognise, flying no flag at all.
